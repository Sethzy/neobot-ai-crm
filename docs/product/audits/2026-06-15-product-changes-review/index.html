1 3 changed paths

Pricing and usage limits feel more launch-ready

Who notices: People checking their plan, message usage, or upgrade options.

Before

Some pages could show internal numbers like 999999 messages, or use harsh fallback labels like Unavailable when billing was not fully connected.

After

Users see clearer plan language: messages used, reset timing, Contact support, or Contact us when checkout is not ready.

What this achieves

This makes the product feel intentional during launch, even if Stripe setup or a paid checkout path is temporarily incomplete.

How the experience looks

A customer can understand whether they are on the right plan without seeing internal placeholders.

Product call: Likely keep. This directly removes rough launch-facing copy.

Settings pages feel more consistent and less unfinished

Who notices: Users managing profile, billing, Telegram, agent behavior, and future settings areas.

Before

Different settings pages used slightly different widths and loading layouts. Future features used labels like Coming soon or Unavailable, which can feel unfinished in production.

After

Settings pages share a calmer layout. Future or inactive channels say Planned, Not connected, or Not configured.

What this achieves

The settings area reads more like a real product surface and less like a collection of temporary pages.

How the experience looks

A user moving between settings pages should feel like they are still in the same product, with clearer expectations about what is available now.

Product call: Likely keep, unless you prefer more explicit Coming soon language.

3 6 changed paths

Customer record pages are tidier and safer to use

Who notices: Users creating contacts, companies, and deals in the CRM.

Before

New records were named New Contact, New Company, or Untitled Deal. Wide tables could push outside the page. Missing custom field settings could break saved views.

After

New records use softer draft names like Client Draft, Company Draft, and Opportunity Draft. Tables stay contained. Views tolerate missing custom field settings.

What this achieves

The CRM feels more deliberate and less brittle, especially during demos and first-time setup.

How the experience looks

A user can add a record and see a normal draft item instead of placeholder-sounding data.

Product call: Likely keep. These are small but visible quality improvements.

Sign-in and account checks are more trustworthy

Who notices: Users confirming email, signing in, or returning to the dashboard.

Before

The app leaned on locally stored session information in a few places.

After

The app asks the account provider for the current user before treating someone as signed in.

What this achieves

This reduces the chance of showing account-only pages based on stale local information.

How the experience looks

Email confirmation and dashboard entry should feel more reliable after sign-in changes.

Product call: Likely keep, but treat as important enough to verify sign-in, sign-out, and email-confirm flows before launch.

6 4 changed paths

General app polish reduces demo rough edges

Who notices: Anyone seeing the product in a launch demo or production trial.

Before

Some screens could produce sideways scroll. Development-only overlays could appear in local demos. A few animations and type details were more distracting than useful.

After

The app is stricter about staying within the viewport, hides development chrome unless explicitly enabled, and uses calmer motion and type spacing.

What this achieves

This makes the app feel more stable and composed during demos.

How the experience looks

Pages should feel less jumpy, less clipped, and less obviously local/dev-only.

Product call: Mostly keep. The only point to review is hiding development chrome, because it affects local debugging unless explicitly enabled.
